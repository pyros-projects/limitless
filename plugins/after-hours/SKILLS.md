# After Hours Skill Set

This is the final top-shelf set for the `after-hours` plugin.

Each skill is meant to feel useful in a live session, not merely clever on a brainstorm board.

## 1. `local-mythology`

**Core move:** turn a project's implicit identity into an explicit lorebook.

**Use when:**
- the project has good instincts but no language for them yet
- features are being added without a clear sense of what the product believes
- naming, tone, or structure is starting to drift
- the team needs a compact articulation of what the project refuses to become

**Output shape:**
- project beliefs
- anti-beliefs
- recurring metaphors
- naming rules
- "this project is / is not" statements

## 2. `taste-distiller`

**Core move:** extract the hidden rules behind why a reference product, interface, or writing style feels right.

**Use when:**
- someone says "I like this, but I can't explain why"
- you want to learn from a reference without copying it literally
- a product needs stronger taste language
- a design direction feels promising but still intuitive and fuzzy

**Output shape:**
- governing rules
- recurring patterns
- density, pacing, tone, and spacing observations
- "constitution of taste" summary

## 3. `failure-postcards`

**Core move:** make future failure modes emotionally concrete.

**Use when:**
- a feature sounds fine on paper but you want to stress it before building
- the team is discussing risks too abstractly
- you want pre-mortem thinking without corporate workshop energy
- a flow needs pressure-testing from the user's point of view

**Output shape:**
- support tickets
- angry emails
- confused user messages
- one-star reviews
- postmortem snippets

## 4. `vibe-checker`

**Core move:** assess whether a product feels like one coherent mind made it.

**Use when:**
- different parts of the product feel tonally inconsistent
- copy, interaction, and UI are pulling in different emotional directions
- a prototype is "fine" but somehow not lovable
- a team wants feedback beyond usability and correctness

**Output shape:**
- coherence review
- tonal mismatches
- emotional arc observations
- recommendations for unifying the feel

## 5. `the-courage-to-delete`

**Core move:** identify what should be removed so the core can breathe again.

**Use when:**
- the product has become crowded or overly ambitious
- a feature set feels bloated
- something valuable is being diluted by additions
- the team needs help choosing what not to do

**Output shape:**
- dilution diagnosis
- candidate deletions
- "what gets stronger if removed" analysis
- leaner product shape

## 6. `naming-as-design`

**Core move:** treat naming as a structural design decision, not editorial cleanup.

**Use when:**
- the same concept is called different things in different places
- the product's mental model feels muddy
- new features are introducing noun sprawl
- the language layer is making the UX harder than it should be

**Output shape:**
- concept map
- naming conflicts
- recommended vocabulary
- mental-model impact of renames

## 7. `ghost-user`

**Core move:** simulate first contact with the product from a cold start and narrate what is confusing, invisible, or assumed.

**Use when:**
- something feels "obvious" to the builders and you want to verify that
- onboarding is thin or nonexistent
- you want a first-time-user pass without formal usability testing
- the surface may be leaking too much author knowledge

**Output shape:**
- first-contact walkthrough
- points of confusion
- hidden assumptions
- friction notes

## Family Resemblance

These are not seven random skills.
They belong together because they all do one of four things:

## Identity

- `local-mythology`
- `naming-as-design`

## Taste

- `taste-distiller`
- `vibe-checker`

## Clarity

- `the-courage-to-delete`
- `ghost-user`

## Pressure-Testing

- `failure-postcards`

## Shared Rule

If a skill creates more noise than clarity, it is being used wrong.

## Pack-Level Warning

These skills should sharpen the work, not delay it.

If you keep chaining them together without making the product, document, or decision more real, stop.

Use one when it clarifies the next move.
Do not use the whole pack as a beautiful way to postpone commitment.
